# Innovation Framework: Platform Team

## Innovation Philosophy

### Why Innovation Matters
- The technology landscape changes faster than any roadmap can anticipate
- Competitors are shipping faster; standing still is moving backward
- Engineers stay and grow where they get to explore, not just execute
- Today's experiment is next year's revenue line or 50% efficiency gain

Innovation here does not mean "shiny side projects." It means a *disciplined
habit* of testing cheap bets against real problems, killing the losers fast,
and scaling the winners. The goal is not more ideas — it's more *validated
learning per dollar and per week*.

### Our Innovation Approach
We allocate engineering capacity across three horizons, adapted from the
classic 70/20/10 model:
- **70% Delivery** — current commitments and roadmap
- **20% Improvement** — making existing systems faster, cheaper, safer
- **10% Exploration** — genuinely new possibilities

This is a *team-level* average, not a per-person rule, and it flexes during
incidents or crunch — but the 10% is protected over a quarter, not sacrificed
the moment delivery gets tight. Protecting it under pressure is the entire test
of whether this framework is real.

## Innovation Programs

### Program 1: Innovation Time (10%)

**Structure:**
- Every engineer gets ~4 hours/week for exploration
- Can be used flexibly (a weekly block or batched into a day)
- No approval needed for the topic

**Expectations:**
- Document learnings (even a paragraph in the experimentation log)
- Share with the team monthly at the showcase
- Explicitly *not* tied to performance evaluation — that would kill it

**Guardrails:**
- Must not jeopardize committed work (it's 10%, not an escape hatch)
- Should relate to our domain (platform, reliability, DX, data)
- Not "more of the same work with a different ticket number"

### Program 2: Hack Days (Quarterly)

**Structure:**
- 2-day quarterly event; normal work paused
- Cross-functional teams welcome (invite product, design, data)
- Demos to the whole org at the end

**Themes (Rotating):**
- Q1: Developer Experience
- Q2: Customer Impact
- Q3: Technical Excellence (reliability, performance, cost)
- Q4: Wild Card

**Support Provided:**
- $500/team budget for tools, APIs, or cloud resources
- Executive sponsors attend demos (signals it matters)
- Top projects get explicit follow-up investment (the crucial part — hack days
  that go nowhere train people to stop trying)

### Program 3: Innovation Proposals

**Structure:**
- Anyone can propose a larger innovation (beyond 10% time)
- Structured one-page proposal: problem, hypothesis, success metric, cost
- Monthly review by tech lead + EM (+ a rotating engineer)

**Criteria for Approval:**
1. Aligned with team vision and one of the three priorities
2. Clear, falsifiable hypothesis and a success metric
3. Reasonable, time-boxed investment (max ~1 engineer-month)
4. A defined path to production *or* a clear learning even if it fails

## Creating Psychological Safety

> Innovation is impossible without psychological safety. People do not take
> risks where failure is punished. This is the foundation everything else rests
> on — and it is built through leader *behavior*, not posters.

### What We Commit To

**As Leaders:**
- Celebrate thoughtful failures publicly, with the learning
- Never punish a good-faith experiment that didn't pan out
- Share our *own* failures first and openly (safety is modeled, not mandated)
- Ask "what did we learn?" and "what would we change?", never "who failed?"

**As a Team:**
- Support each other's experiments; offer help before being asked
- Give specific, kind, actionable feedback
- Share failures as eagerly as successes
- Assume good intent; critique ideas, not people

### Practices

**Failure Retrospectives:**
- Regular blameless sessions on failed experiments and incidents
- Focus on systems and learnings, not individuals
- Documented and shared widely so the whole org learns once

**Experimentation Log:**
- A public log of all experiments — hypothesis, result, learning
- Includes the failures (often the most valuable entries)
- Referenced during planning so we don't repeat dead ends

## Innovation Metrics

> A caution: metrics here are *health signals*, not targets to game. If "10
> experiments/quarter" becomes a quota, people will run 10 trivial experiments.
> Watch the trend and the quality, not just the number.

### Leading Indicators
| Metric | Target |
|---------------------------|--------|
| Innovation time utilized | >80% |
| Hack day participation | ~100% |
| Proposals submitted/quarter | 5+ |
| Experiments run/quarter | 10+ |

### Lagging Indicators
| Metric | Target |
|------------------------------|--------|
| Innovations to production/year | 4+ |
| Efficiency gained from innovations | 20% |
| Team innovation NPS | 60+ |

## Balancing Innovation with Delivery

The hardest part of this framework is honoring it under delivery pressure.
Our rules:
- The 10% is protected at the *quarter* level. We can borrow against it in a
  crunch week, but we repay it — and the tech lead tracks the balance.
- Innovation work has a "definition of done" too: a logged learning. A bet that
  produces no learning is the only kind of failure we discourage.
- When delivery and exploration genuinely conflict, delivery wins *this week* —
  but a quarter where exploration was zero is a leadership failure to flag, not
  a normal outcome.

## Sustaining Innovation

### Monthly Rhythm
- Innovation showcase (30 min, lightweight, demos welcome)
- Proposal reviews
- Metrics/trend review

### Quarterly Rhythm
- Hack days
- Innovation retrospective (is the framework working?)
- Framework adjustment based on what we learned

### Annual Rhythm
- Innovation awards (recognize bold attempts, not just wins)
- Major framework review
- Budget planning for the next year's bets
